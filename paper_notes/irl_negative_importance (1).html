üß† The Core IRL Insight

IRL learns rewards by distinguishing expert demonstrations from realistic alternatives.

The quality of these "realistic alternatives" (negatives) determines how well the reward function captures surgical expertise.

üìö Theoretical Foundation: Why Negatives Matter

1. Maximum Entropy IRL Objective

Loss = -log P(expert_actions | state) + log P(negative_actions | state) Where: P(action | state) ‚àù exp(reward_function(state, action))

The reward function learns to distinguish experts from negatives. If negatives are trivial, the learned distinction is trivial.

2. Contrastive Learning Principle

  • Expert actions get high reward (positive examples)
  • Negative actions get low reward (negative examples)
  • Model learns the decision boundary between good and bad actions

Key insight: The decision boundary is only as sophisticated as your negative examples!

3. Generalization Quality

At test time, the model encounters scenarios similar to your negatives. If you trained on trivial negatives, the model won't handle realistic edge cases.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Pitfalls to Avoid

1. The "Too Easy" Trap

  • Problem: Random or obviously wrong negatives
  • Result: Model learns to avoid nonsense, not make expert decisions
  • Fix: Generate contextually plausible but incorrect actions

2. The "Too Hard" Trap

  • Problem: Negatives that are essentially correct alternatives
  • Result: Model can't distinguish expert from good alternatives
  • Fix: Balance difficulty - some easy, some medium, some hard

3. The "Static Negatives" Trap

  • Problem: Same negatives repeated every training iteration
  • Result: Model overfits to specific negative examples
  • Fix: Dynamic generation with batch-level diversity

4. The "Context-Free" Trap

  • Problem: Negatives that ignore surgical phase/context
  • Result: Model doesn't learn situational appropriateness
  • Fix: Generate phase-aware, context-specific negatives
